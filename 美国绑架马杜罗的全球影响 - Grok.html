The US military operation on January 3, 2026, which resulted in the capture of Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro and his wife Cilia Flores, marks a pivotal moment in contemporary geopolitics. Executed with precision involving Delta Force, over 150 aircraft, and CIA operatives, the raid targeted Maduro's residence in Caracas amid a citywide blackout and bombings of military sites. Maduro, now detained in New York awaiting trial on charges of narcotics terrorism, cocaine importation conspiracy, and illegal weapons possession, faces accusations of leading a regime that facilitated the smuggling of 250 tons of cocaine annually to the US, collaborating with cartels like Sinaloa and Tren de Aragua. His wife is implicated in bribery schemes dating back to 2007, including facilitating drug shipments and accepting payments. This action, framed by the US as judicial enforcement rather than war, has profound ramifications for American foreign policy, global stability, and the balance of power in Latin America.

Historically, US interventions in Latin America have often been justified under the Monroe Doctrine of 1823, which positioned the region as a US sphere of influence to counter European powers. Over time, this evolved into a pattern of regime changes, such as the 1954 overthrow in Guatemala, the 1973 coup in Chile, and the 1989 invasion of Panama to capture Manuel Noriega on similar drug charges. The Maduro operation echoes these, but with heightened stakes in a multipolar world. Under Hugo Chávez (1999-2013), Venezuela nationalized its oil industry in 1976—with full compensation to companies like ExxonMobil—and deepened ties with China and Russia, challenging US dominance. Maduro, succeeding Chávez, faced US sanctions since 2017, exacerbating Venezuela's economic collapse, mass migration (over 7 million refugees), and human rights allegations, including UN reports of crimes against humanity. The 2020 US indictment and $50 million bounty on Maduro set the stage, but the 2026 raid represents an escalation, bypassing international extradition norms.

For the United States, the capture offers tactical gains but substantial risks. President Trump has declared the US "in charge" of Venezuela, emphasizing oil as key to rebuilding the country and lowering global prices. With Venezuela holding 18% of global proven oil reserves (over 300 billion barrels), US firms could auction extraction rights, potentially generating trillions to address domestic fiscal pressures amid a $5 trillion annual financing need. Condemnations have poured in: China's Foreign Ministry decried it as hegemonic, violating sovereignty and threatening Latin American peace; Russia implied criticism through Trump's warnings; Brazil's President Lula called it an "unacceptable affront" and a dangerous precedent; Mexico labeled it a UN Charter violation. Oil markets dipped slightly (US crude to $57/barrel), but long-term disruptions loom if instability persists, affecting China's $105 billion loans and BRICS integration plans for Venezuela.

Humanitarian toll includes civilian casualties from bombings, and the action ignores root causes like sanctions, which exacerbated migration and poverty.
